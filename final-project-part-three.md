# The final data story
Link to Final Shorthand Data Story:
[Link](https://preview.shorthand.com/0Ibvn8fBx9EuIGtk/responsive/desktop)

My final Shorthand story, “How U.S. Air Travel Collapsed and Rebuilt Itself,” presents the rise, collapse, and reconstruction of the U.S. aviation system from 2005–2025, using data from the U.S. Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS) and supporting aviation-industry datasets.

The narrative brings readers through a 15-year boom, the near-total shutdown during COVID, and the system’s dramatic multi-phase rebound. The goal was to tell a story that aviation enthusiasts immediately recognize—but also feel newly informed by through data-driven visuals, hub insights, and airline-strategy comparisons.

# Changes made since Part II
Since completing Part II, I made several structural, visual, and narrative changes to tighten the story flow and better align with my intended aviation-geek audience.

a. Narrowed the Story Focus
Originally, the narrative attempted to cover too many technical aviation topics. After reviewing interview feedback, I removed:
Overly detailed carrier financials
Route-level complexity
Operational metrics too granular for this story

Instead, I focused the core narrative on:
Passenger traffic patterns
Hub dominance shifts
Airline recovery behavior
The three-phase rebuild of the U.S. air network
This created a more cohesive and emotionally compelling structure.

b. Reworked the Visual Order
User feedback indicated that the original visualization order felt cluttered and the treemap was confusing. I reorganized to a three-act structure:

Act I — The Climb: 15 years of growth (2005–2019)
Act II — The Collapse: 4% of passengers, fleets grounded
Act III — The Rebuild: A phased reconstruction of U.S. aviation
This gave the story a natural arc that matches how aviation fans think about timeline-driven industry events.

c. Improved Clarity and Color Use
Interview participants mentioned the treemap felt crowded. I simplified the design to highlight relative market share more clearly.
I also improved color consistency across visuals to make airline comparisons intuitive (same color for each carrier across charts).

d. Strengthened Emotional Connection
Aviation enthusiasts love the system on a personal level. To reflect that:
I added a tribute ending: “To Everyone Who Keeps the Sky Alive—Thank You.”
I added a direct call to action for readers to connect through LinkedIn and GitHub.

e. Accessibility & Readability Enhancements
Increased font sizes for annotations
Added alt-text for all images
Ensured color contrast meets readability standards

## The audience
The intended audience for this project consists of aviation geeks—a passionate and highly engaged community that enjoys exploring:
- Airline networks and schedules
- Hub strength and airport rankings
- Carrier fleet strategies
- Post-pandemic recovery patterns
- Audience Insights Used

Through interviews with three participants (a FlyerTalk reader, a business traveler who follows scheduling trends, and a plane spotter):
- I learned that aviation enthusiasts prefer:
- Clear visuals comparing carrier strategy
- Timeline narratives that show cause-and-effect
- Hub maps and market-share breakdowns
- Simplified charts rather than overwhelming multi-variable graphics
- Adjustments Made Based on Audience
- Removed dense financial or operational metrics
- Added more hub-focused storytelling
- Strengthened annotations explaining WHY patterns matter
- Simplified treemaps and time-series charts
- Added emotional gratitude at the end (aviation culture values community)

## Final design decisions

a. Visual Hierarchy
I intentionally used a top-down structure:
High-level macro trends (passenger volumes)
Airline-level comparisons
Airport-level hub analysis
Emotional dedication + CTA
This mirrors the way aviation geeks mentally organize industry information.

b. Chart Types
Chosen for clarity:
Line charts → long-term trends
Bar charts → airline recovery comparisons
Treemap → market share (simplified after feedback)
Hub ranking bar chart → visually clear and “aviation-geek-friendly”

c. Layout + Flow
Shorthand allowed me to build full-screen visual moments:
A dramatic collapse section showing “4% of passengers”
Large hero images to evoke mood
Section breaks aligned with narrative acts

d. What I Learned
Working through the design process taught me:
More visuals ≠ better visuals
Aviation geeks prefer clarity over complexity
A strong emotional ending elevates a data story
Feedback dramatically improves narrative pacing

# Final thoughts

This project taught me how to combine my personal passion for aviation with data storytelling techniques. My biggest challenges were:
Narrowing the scope (the aviation system is huge)
Ensuring charts were readable to both casual and deep aviation fans
Balancing aesthetics with clarity in Shorthand

What I’m most proud of:
The emotional tone + dedication to aviation workers
The clear three-act structure of the narrative
How the visuals guide readers through a turbulent but inspiring industry story

If I had more time, I would explore:
Route-level network shifts during COVID
Fleet-specific retirements and reactivation timelines
Animated visuals showing hub expansions over time
Overall, this project helped me understand how to craft a compelling story that is deeply rooted in both data and community passion.
